# Identify missing or unknown data values and convert them to NaNs.

def get_missing_flags_for_column(column_name, df=azdias):
    '''
    Takes the name of a column in azdias
    Returns a list of strings used as flags for NaN in that column of azdias
    '''
    col_to_clean = df[column_name]
    flags = feat_info[feat_info['attribute']==col_to_clean.name]['missing_or_unknown']
    flags = flags.get_values()[0]
    flags = flags[1:-1].split(sep=',')
    return flags

def replace_if_flagged(value, flags):
    for flag in flags:
        if str(value) == flag:
            return np.NaN   
    return value

Discussion 2.1: Apply Feature Scaling¶

We have at most 5 missing values in each observation, out of around 70 total So it seems silly to drop the observation, and better to impute. From what I can see, impute only replaces NaN with a simple mean, median or mode of the column or row. The default seems to be to use the row, but instinct says using column norms is the best way to guess the missing value, as meaning is consistent along a column but not along a row. What we really want is something that takes account of the "shape" of the observation relative to the average observation, then acts on the column norm in a way that preserves that shape. But since I didn't know how to do that or even whether it is possible, I imputed based on column mode, that is, each missing value was filled in with the value most frequently found in that column. Scaling was straightforward, with the default values providing the scaling required.

Step 2.3: Interpret Principal Components¶

Now that we have our transformed principal components, it's a nice idea to check out the weight of each variable on the first few components to see if they can be interpreted in some fashion.

As a reminder, each principal component is a unit vector that points in the direction of highest variance (after accounting for the variance captured by earlier principal components). The further a weight is from zero, the more the principal component is in the direction of the corresponding feature. If two features have large weights of the same sign (both positive or both negative), then increases in one tend expect to be associated with increases in the other. To contrast, features with different signs can be expected to show a negative correlation: increases in one variable should result in a decrease in the other.

The most interesting features for each principal component, then, will be those at the beginning and end of the sorted list. Discussion 2.3: Interpret Principal Components¶

Analysis of the first few principal components reveals clear themes.

We might label the first principal component as "Wealth". (The component actually describes lack of wealth, but this still makes variance in wealth its theme). This component is characterised by low income, a high rate of living in buildings with 6 or more families (presumably block of flats), a low rate of living in 1-2 family homes, a high rate of living in communities of more than 700,000, ie a city, and high movement (I think this means moving home often). Having "low financial interest" is also correlated, but I'm not clear what this means.

The second principal component broadly covers "Age and tradition". This component is characterized by higher age, being from a "mainstream" rather than "avant garde" generation, having a "moneysaving" attitude, a high tendency to have a "traditional" or "religious" personality and low tendency to have a "sensual minded" or "event oriented" one. The data also suggested a negative correlation with a "be prepared" approach to finances, which seems anomalous, so I wonder if I am mis-reading or misinterpreting something here.

The third principal component could be called the "hard-nosed factor". Here we find males with a combatitive attitude who are high on being dominant-minded and critical-minded, and low on being socially-minded, family-minded, culturally-minded, uninterested in money, and "dreamful".

Positive and negative values only matter in that they show which features impact in the opposite direction from other features. (You also, of course, need to use them to carefully refer to the data dictionary document, as it turns out in this dataset, high numerical values usually low incidence of the feature. This can get confusing eg FINANZ-MINIMALIST, where a low score means a high affinity with having low financial interest.) Once you have understood the theme of the component, it doesn't matter if you think of it as the kind of person it describes, or the opposite kind, since all it is saying is that a lot of the variation we see in the data is around these features. For instance we could equally well label the third component "Social-mindedness", focusing of the flipside. However we will need to know the direction when interpreting the scores of people within each cluster.
